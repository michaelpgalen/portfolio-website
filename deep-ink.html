Tackling Tattoo Regret UX/UI Case Study

Deep Ink

A responsive web app helping users design unregrettable tattoos by way of color fade predictions, cultural iconography, and smart artist matching.

Executive Summary

Context

Getting a tattoo is an incredibly personal experience. Getting “inked” is a big decision and a massive industry—people spend over 1.65 billion dollars annually on tattoos in the United States alone. A massive 36% of 18–25 year olds and 40% of 26–40 year olds in the U.S. have at least one tattoo. Yet, a surprising 17% of tattoo owners feel some regret.

Assumptions

  • Unanticipated levels of pain lead to regret. Thus, informing users how different body parts correlate with pain level will help tattooists make more confident decisions and reduce regret.
  • Cultural/ancestral connected tattoos are timeless and unregrettable. Thus, helping tattooists design culturally/ancestrally significant tattoos will reduce regret.

Goal

Design a responsive web app that reduces the chance of regret for tattooists.

Summary of Learnings

  • Assumption Invalidated - Pain is not a significant variable in tattoo decisions or regret.
  • Assumption Validated - Tattooists believe designs with cultural/ancestral significance will not be regretted.And, many users could benefit from an easier way to search cultural iconography and design.
  • Discovery - Tattoos changing colors and/or not healing well is a common source of regret, and product features allowing users to predict color changes on their skin tone would have high utility.

My Process

Discovery

Market Research

Competitor Analysis (UX & SWOT)

SWOT Analysis - Tattoodoo
Strengths
  • Huge following on social media.
  • Ability to provide a aggregated booking and consultation platform for worldwide studios/artists.
Weaknesses
  • Some categories are inconsistent.
  • Meaning and significance of tattoo designs is omitted.
Opportunities
  • Focus on first timers and their emotional and thought process behind getting a tattoo.
  • Organize tattoo categories around user motivations and meanings first, tattoo genre’s second.
Threats
  • Trending new app InkHunter with a different objective: leveraging AR technology to enable people to ‘test’ out tattoos on their body on their own time.

Having gained understanding for the current competition and related products, I turned my focus toward the users.

User Research

Interviews

I conducted interviews with 5 participants to better understand the problem space, user needs and context, pain points, and ideate solutions.

Research Goals
  • Determine which apps users enjoy using when gathering inspiration
  • Determine how relevant pain is in picking a tattoo location among users
  • Identify general attitudes towards representing culture/ ancestry in a tattoo
  • Identify reasons for regret
Recruiting
  • Created recruitment emails for the Initial Ask, Confirmation, and Session Reminder.
  • Identified potential participants from my personal network. Potential participants were screened for having tattoos, one cultural/ancestral tattoo, and interested in more tattoos.
  • Reached out via email and phone calls, confirming the first six.

Organizing the Data

I made a profile of each interviewee, organizing and consolidating my notes into four categories.

Theme Analysis

I created affinity maps to find themes in the qualitative data and gain insight into possible product features.

Key Takeaways
  • Users want to personally connect with their artist
  • Users want to be able to refine their image searches more effectively and not feel overwhelmed by irrelevant photos
  • General beliefs that cultural/ancestral tattoos would never be regretted.
  • Users need more information and support regarding colors and how tattoos heal and age
  • Tattoos not healing well and changing color or sharpness is a source of regret/disappointment
Moving Forward

I was surprised to learn how much tattoos can change after healing. I created a survey to discover more about this frustration among tattooists.

Surveys

Research Goals
  • Identify attitudes about type, brand, and color of ink on ones skin
  • Identify frustrations with ink color or sharpness changing over time
  • Determine how much information enthusiasts feel they have access to about ink choice with skin

Data Redefining the Problem

Research data enabled me to narrow the problem space, leading to more focused design solutions. My new working problem statement became: Tattooists need a way to more accurately predict how their tattoo will look after healing, a way to research cultural iconography without feeling overwhelm, and a way to filter images more for their needs.

Point Of View Personas, Journey Maps, User Flows

Who Will Use It?

Using data from my interviews and survey, I created two personas. These served to:

  • Build and maintain empathy for the user
  • Anchor the context of my product throughout the design process
Journey Maps

I then created journey maps to serve as a quick reference throughout my design process, helping keep design decisions aligned with the user’s context, experience and needs.

Journey 1: Design a new colorful tattoo

Ideate

User Flows

Based on my personas and journey maps, I derived key user objectives and did task analysis on each one. Those analyses were then turned into user flows to map all the possible page interactions a visitor might have on the way to their goal.

Site Maps

I was now able to take these user flows and create an initial site map with a navigational structure appropriate for our users. I decided to use a strict hierarchy navigational structure, because the primary features don’t need to connect.

Validating the Site Map with a Card Sort

To validate my site map’s structure and organization, I created an open card sort on OptimalWorkshop and recruited 5 participants.I used Optimal Workshop’s Similarity matrix to find patterns within this small data set.

Key learnings were applied to create site map version 2. Changes made based on the card sort are in RED.

Design Wireframes & Prototypes

Lo-fidelity Rapid Prototyping

With user flows and a validated site map, I began sketching lo-fi wireframes and prototypes using pencil and paper to convey the high level user experience. This helped visualize the user flow in screens, and get the basic navigation and button requirements.

Mid- & Hi-Fidelity Clickable Prototypes

I created mid- then hi-fidelity clickable prototypes, incorporating more detail each iteration, yet still saving a lot of the copy, images, design system colors for later. The allowed me to focus on usability.

Testing

Usability Testing Recruitment • Test Facilitation • Data Analysis • Reporting

To validate the design decisions in my prototypes thus far, I conducted usability tests with 6 participants. Each test involved basic demographics, scenario tasks, and follow-up questions.

Goals and objectives

  • Evaluate the app’s learnability for new users
  • Observe and measure how many and what types of errors users make when completing basic functions
  • Evaluate what new users understand the value of the app to be

Organizing the Data Affinity Mapping & A Rainbow Spreadsheet

I Created an affinity map to organize the test session insights. Insights were consolidated into a rainbow spreadsheet, allowing me to visualize the data and prioritize the top 5 usability issues.

Data Influencing Design Top 5 issues + Recommendations + Evidence

Applied Learnings

Onboarding

Simplified and distilled on-boarding graphics, decreasing the cognitive load and better informing the user.

Color prediction flow

Added sample design options to the color prediction flow, empowering the user and informing them what to expect in a prediction. Now users could anticipate and have some control over the display of their color predictions.
